Prior work

There are several publications that highlight ethnicity and gender and issues of diversity at Stanford and in particular the School of Engineering.

The first resource is the Undergraduate Student Profile published by Stanford annually. This showcases a remarkable bar chart with 2 values: 50% men and 50% women. No interactions between gender and race or ethnicity is reported.

The Stanford Daily published a graphic showing the gender ratio at Stanford in the 2015-2016 academic year (Li, 2016). The graph presents a useful snapshot at one particular moment in time.

A previous project called Stanford Visualized aims to take a quantitative look at Stanford's enrollment over a long period of time. The data there covers 1963-2014, spanning five decades. It contains number of enrolled students in a given major for every year. The data highlights how majors evolve over time, tracking both undergraduate and graduate students. It is possible to compare majors by looking at enrolled students by gender, but no race or ethnicity data is available.

Dataset

The dataset covers years 2010-2018 and shows Undergraduate degrees awarded by ethnicity and gender. The data is collected from the American Society for Engineering Education website where it is published reported by Stanford. The list of sources contains links to the College Profiles for Stanford University. The reports break down degrees award by Ethnicity and Gender; the same terminology is maintained here and ethnicity as a term is maintained.

The Ethnicity variable has a certain overlap with race, but race cannot be derived from ethnicity in this data set. For example, US citizens of Asian American descent are reported as Asian Americans, while foreign students from Asian countries will be reported under Nra - nonresident aliens. Similarly, Blk - Black includes African Americans, but not black students who are not US citizens.

With regards to Gender, the report only contains male/female breakdown. This is maintained here.

Project

The project builds on the data reported from Stanford to create this tool. The main goal is to allow comparing different majors and how those evolve over time.

The charts are built using Chart.js. An inspiration for the graph was drawn from the book Beautiful evidence (Tufte, 2006). Particularly, when a limited number of lines are shown, each line is decorated with precise numbers for that indicator variable and year. Further, hovering with a mouse or touching a year (on a mobile device) shows a list of the values for that year.

The color scheme of the graph was selected to maintain usability by color-blind people (Tol, 2018). This has the added benefit that the charts produced will be legible and readable in print too.
